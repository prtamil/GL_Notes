The philosophy of **Stoicism** teaches a disciplined way of living: see reality clearly, prepare the mind for difficulty, focus only on what lies within your power, and act with purpose in the present moment. The following five Latin reminders capture this mindset as a simple daily guide.

**Memento Mori — Remember you will die.**  
This phrase reminds us that life is temporary and time is limited. Far from being pessimistic, this awareness clarifies what truly matters. When we remember that our time is finite, we become less distracted by trivial worries and unnecessary fears. Mortality brings perspective, encouraging us to live deliberately and to value each moment.

**Amor Fati — Love your fate.**  
Life inevitably presents circumstances we cannot control: setbacks, criticism, uncertainty, and change. Stoicism teaches that resisting reality only creates frustration. Instead, we should accept events as part of the natural order. “Amor Fati” means more than simple acceptance—it suggests embracing what happens and using every circumstance as material for growth and learning.

**Premeditatio Malorum — Expect difficulties.**  
Stoic thinkers such as Seneca and Marcus Aurelius practiced mentally preparing for obstacles before they occurred. By anticipating potential problems—delays, criticism, failures, or challenges—the mind becomes resilient rather than surprised. When difficulties arise, they are seen not as disruptions but as part of the expected path.

**Potestatem Intende, Cetera Dimitte — Focus on your power; release the rest.**  
A central Stoic teaching, emphasized by Epictetus, is the distinction between what lies within our control and what does not. Our actions, choices, and attitudes belong to us; outcomes, opinions, and external events often do not. Peace of mind comes from directing effort only toward what we can influence and letting go of everything else.

**Nunc Age, Nihil Expectans — Act now, expecting nothing.**  
After aligning the mind with reality and focusing on what is within our control, the final step is action. This phrase encourages acting in the present moment without attachment to results. Effort is placed fully in the task itself rather than in imagined outcomes. By acting without expectation, we remain steady regardless of success or failure.

Together these five principles form a practical Stoic mindset: remember life is short, accept the course of events, prepare for difficulty, focus only on what you control, and act decisively in the present moment. Practiced regularly, these ideas cultivate clarity, resilience, and purposeful living.
