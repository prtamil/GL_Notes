The philosophy of **Stoicism** teaches a simple but powerful way to live: see reality clearly, prepare the mind for difficulty, focus only on what is within your control, and act with discipline. The following five Latin reminders capture this mindset as a daily mental practice.

**Memento Mori — Remember you will die.**  
This idea places life in its proper perspective. Human life is temporary, and time is limited. Remembering mortality does not create despair; instead it clarifies priorities. When we remember that life is finite, we stop wasting energy on trivial worries, status, or unnecessary fear. The awareness of death sharpens attention toward what truly matters.

**Amor Fati — Love your fate.**  
Life inevitably contains events beyond our control: setbacks, criticism, loss, and uncertainty. Rather than resisting these realities, Stoicism teaches acceptance. “Amor Fati” means more than tolerating events; it means embracing them as part of the natural order. Every difficulty becomes material for growth, training the mind to remain steady in changing circumstances.

**Premeditatio Malorum — Expect difficulties.**  
Stoic thinkers like Seneca and Marcus Aurelius practiced mentally anticipating obstacles before they occurred. By imagining challenges in advance—delays, criticism, failure, or uncertainty—the mind becomes prepared rather than surprised. When difficulties eventually arise, they feel familiar rather than shocking.

**Potestatem Intende, Cetera Dimitte — Focus on your power; release the rest.**  
One of the central teachings of Stoicism, emphasized by Epictetus, is the distinction between what we control and what we do not. Our actions, judgments, and efforts belong to us; outcomes, opinions, and external events do not. Peace of mind comes from directing attention only toward what lies within our power and letting go of everything else.

**Carpe Diem Sine Spe — Seize the day without expectation.**  
Once the mind is aligned with reality and focused on what it can control, the final step is action. Acting “without expectation” means working without attachment to results. Effort is placed in the present moment, not in imagined outcomes. Today becomes the field of action.

Together these five principles create a disciplined mindset: remember life is short, accept reality, prepare for difficulty, focus only on what you control, and act fully in the present. This simple cycle reflects the spirit of Stoic philosophy and offers a practical guide for living with clarity, resilience, and purpose.
